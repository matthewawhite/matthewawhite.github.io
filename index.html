We all continue to deny the ugly in the world (Videos)

By Matthew White, November 3rd, 2016

        

Racist ad depicting the superiority of great white explorers over the "uncivilized" Gold Dust Twins

T

he concept of racism in the modern day seems ridiculous. It's 2016, racism should be non-existent, yet media is a constant reminder we do not live in a perfect world. Most of us consume enormous sums of media, and hardly ever pick up on any message that may be racist. This can easily be proven by paying more attention to the commercials that we all tend to loosely pay attention to.

 

If you were born in the 90s, the idea of blatantly racism commercials may seem to be a figment of imagination. While commercials since the 90s have often included racist elements, those of extreme racism have existed but are few and far between and tend to be taken off air quickly. However, this was not true not too long ago in the 20th century.

 

"Asian Jell-O Commercial"

           

           

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

In this short commercial, Asians are being depicted in a stereotypical and racist manner. First of all, the voice of the narrator is speaking in a manner that mocks Chinese accents. For example, the narrator pronounces the word "bring" as "bling" when the mother brings a spoon over to the child. At the same time, the commercial makes the Chinese seem inferior by "demonstrating" the difficulty one may have with eating Jell-O with chopsticks, and then regards spoons as a "great western invention", another inaccurate claim. This commercial is one of many from the era that could easily be regarded as racist.

 

While it may be expected that some older commercials depict racist attitudes, this can also be observed in more modern media as well. Even during the Super Bowl, what many consider to be the pinnacle of commercial media, racist depictions may arise. This includes commercials aired during Super Bowls since the beginning of the 2000s. Not only do racist commercials continue to be made, they racist depictions of Asians seem to not have changed as well.

 

"SalesGenie Super Bowl Commercial"

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

This video displays the racist views of Asians that are still present within society, and was originally aired in the 2008 Super Bowl. The obvious aspects of racism present within this video are the voices of the characters as well as the stereotypical Chinese names of the characters.

 

Not only are the depictions of Asian characters often stereotypical or racist within commercial media, many other minorities are often the subject of racism. For example, African Americans as well as Asians of darker skin are criticized. These ads can range from displaying stereotypes of black people, as in this KFC commercial, to what is sometimes called the most racist commercial in history, shown below.

Do I even need to comment on the issues with this commercial? I think that the video itself proves my point that racism still exists within our society and especially within commercial ads.

 

Not only is Western society struggling with this issue, but the whole world is subject to blatant racism in commercial ads. This is particularly obvious within this Chinese commercial depicted an ad for laundry detergent.

 

"Qiao bi laundry detergent"

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

This commercial holds nothing back in displaying racism by claiming the inferiority of black men for a white Asian male. It is made evident by this commercial that racial prejudice is not segregated to only some parts of the world, but exists everywhere.

 

When children are growing up, they are exposed to this wide array of racial prejudice within media. While the commercials may not be as extreme as those shown above, racial prejudice can still be displayed in minute details. Consequently, by the time the children grow up, they will have formed opinions of other races based off of a variety of media including commercial ads. This has been easily proven by observing children who have not been exposed to much media do not display prejudice against other races.

 

Through this constant barrage of prejudice displayed in media, a social hierarchy has formed within society. While this social hierarchy can often depend on where in the world one is, it is often obvious who is at the top of the social order. It is critical that the world change this ugly that exists in the deep reaches of the internet and media, but much must change. The cycle will continue as long as children are subjected to media that portrays social stereotypes. Consequently, directors of commercial ads must keep in mind that their influence goes beyond getting people to buy their product. Directors have the power to influence people in a multitude of ways and some directors are partially responsible for creating the social hierarchy that exists. If any director or actor in the commercial business or involved with other forms of media is reading this, I would like to ask you to thoroughly consider the message you may be sending to your viewers beyond selling your product.

 

Media have been able to change the world for the worse at times, so why not try to reverse the trend. These commercial ads that project racial prejudice have harmed society, but commercials also have the power to change this. Displaying a wide variety of races in a positive manner will reinforce the views children already have that all races are equal. In a decade or two I hope that I will be able to write about how commercials have helped racial equality in the world, instead of this more depressing article regarding the horrible views that are present in society.

 

To those who read this and think, "I never see racism within commercial ads" and simply deny any racism that may exist, I ask you to follow my next suggestion. The next time you watch TV, instead of leaving the room, getting on your phone, or ignoring the TV, try instead to pay careful attention to what the commercials are saying and showing. It would not take long to realize that prejudice exists and commercials are often the perpetrators of this prejudice.
